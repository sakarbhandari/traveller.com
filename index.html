Devchuli Dada

Where the Mountains Meet the Spirit:

Devchuli Dada (also known as Devchuli Hill) in Nepal is a beautiful, lesser-known travel destination rich in natural beauty, culture, and spirituality. Starting a travel blog centered on Devchuli Dada could be a great niche—especially if you want to share local stories, hiking experiences, cultural insights, or eco-tourism content.

I set off early in the morning with a backpack, a bottle of water, and a curious heart. The trail was steep in parts, gentle in others, lined with sal forests, chirping birds, and occasional shrines hidden in the greenery. I passed a few friendly locals — one old man smiled and said, “Sabai thik huncha dada ma” (Everything feels right on the hill).

After hours of hiking, the summit opened up to a panoramic view of the Annapurna range, glowing under the soft sun. I sat there, wind brushing against my face, feeling small — in a good way. I wasn’t just looking at nature; I was part of it.

It taught me patience. Humility. And the power of quiet places. This blog is my digital notebook — I’ll be sharing more hikes, stories, people, and reflections. If you're planning to visit Devchuli, here are a few quick tips:

Quick Travel Tips:

- Time: October–March (clear skies, mild weather).

- Gear: Good hiking shoes, light jacket, snacks, water.

- Local Stay:Try a Tharu homestay near Daldale or Kawasoti.

- Guide: Not required but helpful if you want to explore deeper trails.

Pumdikot

Serenity Above the Clouds

Just a short drive from the bustling lakeside town of Pokhara, Nepal, lies Pumdikot—a tranquil hilltop destination blending spirituality, panoramic mountain views, and peaceful nature walks. On my recent trip, this lesser-known spot turned out to be a highlight, offering a peaceful retreat above the clouds.

Reaching Pumdikot is an adventure in itself. From Pokhara, it's roughly a 45-minute ride, winding uphill through lush forests and small villages. You can either hire a cab or, for the adventurous, hike from World Peace Pagoda, which takes about 45 minutes to an hour.

The centerpiece of Pumdikot is the majestic Shiva statue, standing 51 feet tall atop a symbolic Kailash Parvat. The site is not just visually striking—it carries deep spiritual energy. The atmosphere here is serene, with prayer flags fluttering in the wind and visitors silently taking in the views. Around the statue is a beautifully designed viewpoint area, complete with symbolic representations of Shiva’s Damru (drum) and Trishul (trident). The site also includes a small replica of Kedarnath Temple, adding to the religious significance.

What truly sets Pumdikot apart is the 360-degree panoramic view. On a clear day, you can see the snow-capped Annapurna and Machhapuchhre (Fishtail) mountains, the Phewa Lake shimmering below, and the entire Pokhara valley stretched out like a painting. Sunrise and sunset here are unforgettable. I arrived just before sunset, and watching the golden light touch the mountain peaks while the valley slowly lit up was a magical experience.

Things to Know Before You Go

- Best time to visit: October to April for clear mountain views.

- Entrance: There’s no major fee, though donations are welcome for temple maintenance.

- Accessibility: Elderly visitors or those with mobility issues can drive directly to the viewpoint.
